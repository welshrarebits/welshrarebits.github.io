Work / Case Study — 01

Re-imagining the GDS ‘Good Practice Guides’

An investigation into the elasticity of identity verification.

Client Department for Work & Pensions (DWP)
Role Senior Identity & Trust Specialist
Area Identity & Trust
Year 2022–2020
Back

01

The Problem

In 2012 GDS published its series of Good Practice Guides (GPGs), for providers of identity assurance for government services:

  • GPG 43: Requirements for Secure Delivery of Online Public Services (RSDOPS)
  • GPG 44: Authentication Credentials in Support of HMG Online Services
  • GPG 45: Validating and Verifying the Identity of an Individual

In my role as both a Product Manager and later as an Identity & Trust Specialist in Policy, these became critical documents for me to understand, reference, and perhaps most importantly articulate to others.

However, GPG 45 alone weighs in at 33 pages (8972 words), including a 29 page sub section with 32 individual ID profiles presented as tabular data. So I think it’s fair to say these were not the most accessible reads. Even by their own admission the Cabinet Office recognised (via their own website) that “these guides are necessarily quite technical”.

I had already seen a couple attempts to re-frame this information, for example the digital consultancy Hippo, who opted for a Lego bricks analogy (see below).

02

Approach

We ran a series of contextual enquiry sessions with caseworkers across three regional offices, shadowing daily workflows and identifying the points at which the designed service diverged from lived reality.

“The most important thing was making the invisible visible — mapping what caseworkers actually did versus what policy intended.”

Service blueprint mapped across five departments, co-created with frontline caseworkers.

Workshop synthesis sessions brought together policy leads, product managers, and frontline staff to pressure-test the emerging recommendations before they were codified into guidance.

03

Outcome

The resulting practice guides were adopted across four government departments and informed the GDS updated guidance on end-to-end service design. A framework for measuring service quality at the seams between departments was piloted with two agencies.
