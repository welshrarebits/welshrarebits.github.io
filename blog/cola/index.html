Innovation or Irritation?

In Oct last year, Coca-Cola announced its ‘attached cap’ innovation in the UK. Now, I’ll be honest with you, this news largely passed me by. It wasn’t until this modification was expanded to 500ml bottles that it even appeared on my radar — or to be more accurate, passed silently beneath it.

The ‘innovation’ in question is attempting to solve a debatable problem: how to keep waste bottles and caps together to promote recycling. While a noble cause, I’d always been brought up to squeeze the air out of a bottle, then reattach the lid to create a vacuum, solving both the lid and cap problem and reducing space in the recycling bin.

What is the problem you are trying to solve?

Is this ‘solution’ truly solving a problem that needs addressing? With Coke already voted worst plastic polluter four years running by the nonprofit coalition Break Free From Plastic, wouldn’t research into plastic alternatives be a better use of time, resources, and money?

User Experience / User Needs

I’m happy to be proven wrong but I can’t believe for a second that user feedback indicated this was an improvement on the previously unattached caps. In fact, I’d love to read the user research behind it.

Form (should) follow function

Whilst attaching the cap to the bottle does indeed keep the two items together, it also becomes an annoying appendage that makes drinking from the bottle unnecessarily fussy. Most of the people I spoke to said they ended up ripping the cap from the bottle regardless — leaving a serrated edge that’s uncomfortable to drink from.

Sailing dangerously close to ‘old man yells at cloud’ territory

Good design should be silent

When ring pulls moved from ‘pull tabs’ to ‘stay-ons’ in 1989, I don’t recall a marketing campaign or explanations printed on the top of cans. They just worked — they did not impact the drinking experience but still managed to hit the business objective of reducing litter.

It does give me a certain degree of schadenfreude when I see companies the size of Coke getting design decisions this wrong.

I’d definitely be interested in others’ thoughts on this, particularly from a product design or comms perspective, but also from a ‘business vs user needs’ standpoint too.
